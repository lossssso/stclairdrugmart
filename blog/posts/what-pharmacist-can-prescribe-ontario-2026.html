What Can a Pharmacist Prescribe in Ontario? The 2026 Minor Ailments Guide

Updated June 20, 2026 · 7 min read · Minor Ailments

Here's something a lot of people in Toronto still don't realize: you no longer need a doctor for many everyday health problems. Under Ontario's Minor Ailments Program, a pharmacist can now assess and prescribe for 28 common conditions — no appointment, no referral, and for eligible patients, the assessment is covered by OHIP. This guide walks through the full list and exactly how it works at St. Clair Drug Mart Pharmacy in St. Clair West.

What is the Minor Ailments Program?

A "minor ailment" is a common health condition that's well understood, short-term, and can be safely managed with self-care and, when needed, a prescription. Instead of waiting days for a doctor or hours at a walk-in clinic, you can have a trained pharmacist assess your symptoms on the spot and, if appropriate, prescribe treatment right away. It's faster for you and takes pressure off Ontario's busy clinics and emergency rooms.

The full list: 28 conditions your pharmacist can treat

As of 2026, Ontario pharmacists can assess and prescribe for all of the following:

  • Eyes: pink eye (conjunctivitis), dry eye
  • Nose & allergies: allergic rhinitis (seasonal allergies), nasal congestion
  • Mouth: oral thrush, cold sores, canker sores
  • Pain: mild headache, period (menstrual) pain, sprains & strains
  • Skin: acne, eczema/dermatitis, dandruff, impetigo, ringworm, warts, athlete's foot, jock itch, calluses & corns
  • Digestive: heartburn/GERD, hemorrhoids
  • Infections & pests: urinary tract infection (UTI), vaginal yeast infection, head lice, pinworms
  • Bites: insect bites & stings, tick bites (including prevention of Lyme disease where appropriate)
  • Pregnancy: nausea and vomiting in pregnancy

That's 28 conditions in total — and Ontario has announced more conditions and vaccines are being added in 2026, so the list keeps growing.

How an assessment works — step by step

  1. You walk in or book online. No referral required. Same-day is usually no problem.
  2. The pharmacist asks a few questions about your symptoms, your health history, and any medications you take.
  3. You get a recommendation. That might be a prescription, an over-the-counter product, or — if your symptoms need a physician — a referral to the right place.
  4. You leave with a plan, often in 10–15 minutes.

Does it cost anything?

For most minor ailments, the assessment is covered by OHIP for patients with a valid Ontario health card — you pay nothing for the consultation. If a medication is prescribed, normal prescription coverage applies (your drug plan, or the cost of the medication itself). We'll always be upfront about any cost before you commit.

When a pharmacist will send you to a doctor

The program is designed with safety first. If your symptoms are severe, unusual, lasting longer than expected, or could be a sign of something more serious, the pharmacist's job is to recognize that and refer you to a physician or urgent care. You're not bypassing the system — you're being routed to the right level of care more quickly.
